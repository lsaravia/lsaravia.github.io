Ecosystems are made of thousands of different species that interact and transfer energy from autotrophs to top predators. These form interaction networks that adapt and change when environmental conditions fluctuate, and now with climate change there is more interest on how these ecological webs will survive or adapt. 

This has been studied using models since the main results are from R. May[^1] in 1972 using local stability analysis. Local stability is the ability of an equilibrium point to recover after a small change in the population abundances.

In fact, we know that real ecological networks, have the ability to resist small fluctuations, because there are always demographic fluctuations, seasonal fluctuations, and other kinds of environmental fluctuations.

So the problem is what happens if environmental change causes changes in other parameters, like growth rates? 

There is another interesting concept that was also analysed quickly after May's publication but that was mostly ignored by ecologists: equilibrium points can be locally stable, but we need to be sure that all the species populations are greater than zero, this was called feasibility [^2].  Thus if climatic change modify the parameters for species we need to know if the feasibility condition holds. Following this we need to know how large is the region of the parameter space were the feasibility condition keeps. This was called the feasibility domain [^3].

So what matters for species coexistence is that the system is locally stable and also the size of the feasibility domain, because the bigger the size the better the ecosystem can cope with changes. One definition that improves this is global stability: a fixed point is globally stable if the system eventually return to it, starting from any positive initial condition within a finite domain.

This is much more stronger than local stability and the combination of global stability with feasibility has been termed: structural stability [^3]. 

There are new studies that obtained interesting results about feasibility, local stability, and structural stability. 

Feasibility was analysed assuming global stability, and the results were obtained using communities that have a random structure of interactions [^4]. We know that communities are not random [^6], but it sets a baseline null model to compare. In the case of random communities the mean of interaction strengths and the number of species are the most important factors for feasibility (constrained to stable situations). For competition and predation the mean could be negative, then the larger the system is, the smaller is the set of conditions leading to coexistence, while for mutualism the mean is positive so larger systems have greater feasibility domain [^4]. In terms of network properties the relevant factor is connectance, and other properties have a minimal influence.

What happens when we add a real community structure: mutualistic communities follow closely their random counterpart [^5] but food webs have a smaller feasibility domains that means that some properties of its structure constrain it, which properties, we still do not know [^4].

On the other hand, as feasibility and stability are independent properties, new findings about stability can shed some light on the overall picture [^6]. Using the cascade model to generate food webs it was determined that the average interaction strength has little effect on stability and that size and connectance have a stronger effect.  

To link these kind of studies with natural ecological network the problem is parametrization, how do we know that the region of the parameters used correspond to the real systems?
